## 1. What Is a POS System and What Does It Consist Of

When we hear "POS," we mistakenly assume it's just the device that cashiers or waiters use to swipe a credit card to charge for a service or product.

While that is a small part of it, a POS system is far more than that. It's a system that connects all information from different parts of a business and turns it into a clear, usable result.

A POS system is the link between the end customer and the owner, manager, and salesperson.

A modern POS system consists of **hardware and software components** that are integrated and allow users to track business processes.

### POS System Hardware

POS hardware typically includes:

- some type of computer
- a printer for printing fiscal receipts and invoices
- additional equipment

The hardware component is very diverse and fully adaptable to the needs and scale of the business.

For example, larger companies that issue a high volume of receipts most commonly use **Windows or Mac computers** connected to printers. Smaller companies with limited business space or a priority on mobility often use small portable devices.

**Android devices** that look like smartphones with an integrated printer are currently very popular on the market.

The specifics of each industry bring specific additional equipment, so we often see:

- barcode scanners for fast item entry in supermarkets, to avoid creating long lines of dissatisfied customers
- kitchen display systems for communication between waiters and chefs in larger restaurants, so food reaches guests faster
- devices with longer battery life for field salespeople, so they don't have to worry about charging frequently
- self-service kiosks in fast food restaurants to reduce queues at the counter

These are just some examples showing how specialized and tailored to user needs POS hardware has become today.

### POS System Software

The software component is the **brain of the POS system**. It handles all processes in the POS system.

It typically consists of two parts:

- back-office
- sales interface

#### Back-Office

The back-office is the management component designed for managers, and through it you can:

- manage products (price lists, codes or barcodes, tax rates, names)
- manage inventory (receiving and dispatching goods, calculations, material accounting)
- manage employees (working hours, shifts, payroll, privileges)
- generate reports and analytics (revenue reports by various categories)
- manage customers (CRM)
- integrate with delivery apps, accounting systems, etc.

#### Sales Interface

The sales interface is used by salespeople, waiters, and cashiers.

Its main functions are:

- processing transactions (sales, advance payments, receipt cancellations, proforma invoices, invoices, e-invoices)
- receipt fiscalization

## 2. How a POS System Works

A modern POS system functions as an integrated platform that connects hardware (computers, screens, barcode scanners, thermal and regular printers, cash drawers, portable devices, etc.) with software.

During a sale, items are identified by scanning barcodes, entering special codes, or pressing item buttons. The software then applies predefined price lists, tax rates, and discounts, updates inventory levels in real time, and sends a request to issue a fiscal receipt.

In the background, the software tracks all these transactions, which means various reports can be created at any time and a large number of business processes can be managed.

POS systems can integrate with ERP, accounting software, CRM, loyalty programs, and e-commerce platforms.

## 3. From Cash Registers to POS Systems - Cloud as the Game Changer

In recent years, especially after the introduction of electronic fiscalization in the region, there has been a massive adoption of POS systems and a gradual disappearance of traditional cash registers.

Computers and Android fiscal devices have replaced old cash registers and enabled much more than just recording sales.

Today, POS systems track a large number of business processes.

Another major trend is the transition of POS systems to **cloud solutions**.

A cloud POS system stores all its data on the internet and can be accessed from any device with an internet connection.

All information from a cloud POS system is available in real time, from anywhere, at any moment. Even if the device you're currently using stops working, you simply pick up a new one and all data remains saved - you continue working right where you left off.

## 5. Types of POS Systems by Industry

20 years ago, virtually everyone used the same cash registers, regardless of industry.

Today, the situation is completely different.

POS systems are often developed specifically for a particular industry.

For example:

- hair salon - a small Android device with an integrated printer
- coffee bar - a computer with a water-resistant touchscreen
- large supermarkets - multi-line barcode scanners and fast software

Due to different user needs, POS systems have become highly personalized.

They are most commonly divided into:

- POS systems for hospitality
- POS systems for retail

## 6. POS Systems for Hospitality

Hospitality POS systems are optimized through their hardware and software for faster order processing and better communication between staff.

### Hospitality Hardware

Hospitality hardware typically includes:

- all-in-one computer with a touchscreen
- receipt printer
- kitchen printer
- bar printer
- card payment terminals
- kitchen display screen
- tablets for remote orders
- waiter card readers

Hospitality hardware is often resistant to water, dust, and damage due to the high frequency of use.

### Hospitality Software

The software is built for speed and clarity.

The sales interface often features:

- a graphical table layout divided into zones
- large buttons with product images
- product categorization
- clearly differentiated buttons for the most common actions

The back-office typically includes:

- product and price list management
- inventory management
- food and drink ingredient management
- advanced revenue reports
- CRM features

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions About POS Systems

### Is a POS system the same as a cash register?

A POS system is, among other things, also a cash register. Beyond that function, it has many additional capabilities that enable tracking of goods and service transactions.

### Can a POS system work on my computer or smartphone?

The software component of a POS system is often cross-platform and can run on:

- Windows computers
- Mac computers
- Android smartphones

### What is e-fiscalization?

E-fiscalization refers to the automatic sending of all transaction receipts from your POS system to the Tax Administration server for validation and fiscalization. It is mandatory in many countries across the region.

### Do I need the internet for a POS system to work?

Due to e-fiscalization requirements, an internet connection must be available on the POS system.

You don't need to be constantly online, as most systems have an **offline mode**, but internet must be available at least once a day for synchronization with the Tax Administration.

### How much does a POS system cost?

A POS system consists of hardware and software.

Typical prices are:

Hardware:

- most affordable Android devices: around **$250**
- more advanced systems: several thousand dollars

Software:

- **$12-20 per month** for retail
- **$25-50 per month** for hospitality

### What should I consider when buying a POS system?

Pay attention to:

- hardware warranty
- licensed software that follows legal regulations
- the reputation of the company selling the POS system
- cloud functionality
- quality of technical support

### How difficult is it to learn to use a POS system?

Working with a POS system shouldn't be difficult.

The manufacturer's goal is to make the software intuitive and easy to use.

Training typically takes:

- about **30 minutes** for the back-office
- about **20 minutes** for the sales interface
